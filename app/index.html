Chapter 1

What is gold, exactly?

Gold is a metal — but it's also one of the oldest forms of money in human history. For thousands of years, civilizations used gold to trade, pay soldiers, and store wealth. Kings hoarded it. Empires were built on it.

Today, gold doesn't back our currency the way it once did. But it's still treated as a store of value — something people trust to hold its worth over time, especially when other things (like stocks, or even cash) are shaky.

In fact, in arguably the first written law ever recorded — the 4,000-year-old Code of Hammurabi — the word for money literally meant a defined weight of gold or silver. For most of written history, "money" has always meant precious metal.

The key idea: Gold is valuable because people believe it's valuable — and they've believed that for 5,000 years. That kind of trust is surprisingly durable.
🏛️

Ancient money

Used as currency since at least 600 BC. The Roman Empire paid its soldiers in gold coins.

🔬

Chemically special

Gold doesn't rust, tarnish, or corrode. A gold coin buried for 2,000 years still looks like gold.

⚖️

Finite supply

All the gold ever mined would fill about 3.5 Olympic swimming pools. You can't just print more.

🔒

No counterparty risk

Physical gold isn't someone else's debt or promise. A stock can go to zero. A bar of gold can't go bankrupt.

That price moves based on supply and demand — just like a house, a stock, or a loaf of bread. Here's what pushes it up or down:

📈

Fear & uncertainty push it up

When people are worried about the economy, inflation, or geopolitical instability, they flee to gold. It's the classic "safe haven" asset.

💵

A weak dollar pushes it up

Gold is priced in dollars. When the dollar weakens (which it does over time by design), gold gets more expensive in dollar terms — so its price rises.

🏦

Central bank buying pushes it up

Countries like China, Russia, and India have been buying gold aggressively to reduce their dependence on US dollars. China is now the world's biggest gold buyer.

📉

High interest rates can push it down

Gold doesn't pay interest or dividends. When rates are high, safe bonds become more attractive, temporarily reducing gold's appeal.

Perspective: In 2000, gold was ~$280/oz. Today it's over $5,000. That's an 18x increase in 25 years — far outpacing inflation, savings accounts, and most bonds. Gold has risen in value against every single national currency in the world since the year 2000.
Chapter 3

Why cash quietly loses value — and gold doesn't

You know how things just seem to cost more every year? That's not a coincidence. It's a feature of how modern money works.

Since 1971, every currency in the world — the dollar, the pound, the euro — has been what's called a fiat currency. That means it's just paper (or digits) backed by government promises, with nothing physical behind it. Governments can print as much as they want.

"All these pieces of paper are issued with as much solemnity and authority as if they were of pure gold or silver..." — Marco Polo, describing Chinese paper money in the 13th Century AD

History isn't kind to fiat currencies. A study of 775 different ones found that not a single one has held its value over the long term. On average, a fiat currency lasts just 27 years. The reasons they fail: hyperinflation, wars, or governments simply printing too much.

Gold, by contrast, can't be printed. Its supply grows by only about 1–2% per year through mining. That scarcity is exactly why it has preserved purchasing power across centuries.

Gresham's Law: bad money drives out good

There's an old economic principle that says when two forms of money circulate, people spend the "bad" (less valuable) one and save the "good" (more valuable) one. This is why, when the US replaced silver coins with cheaper metal coins in 1965, people quietly hoarded the silver ones and spent the new ones. The same instinct applies to gold — when people sense their currency is losing value, they quietly convert some of it to gold.

Chapter 4

Exter's Pyramid: where gold sits in the financial world

In the 1960s, an American economist named John Exter — who worked at the Federal Reserve and founded the Central Bank of Sri Lanka — created a visual model to rank all asset classes by risk. He called it the Inverse Pyramid.

The idea is simple: assets at the top are the most risky (they can collapse to zero). Assets at the bottom are the most reliable. In a financial crisis, money flows down the pyramid as people flee risky assets for safe ones.

Notice where our assets sit in this pyramid. Our property is in the middle. Our stocks are near the top. Our cash is near the bottom — but still above gold. Gold alone sits at the very foundation, with no counterparty risk.

"Gold will be the single best investment to own." — John Exter, Federal Reserve economist & founder of the Central Bank of Sri Lanka
What this means for us: In a financial crisis — when stocks fall and property loses value — gold typically moves in the opposite direction. It's the asset class that survives precisely because it doesn't depend on anyone's promise to be worth something.

Chapter 6

How much gold should we own? (The "10% myth" explained)

You'll often hear financial advisors casually say "keep 5–10% in gold." But research from the CPM Group — one of the most respected precious metals research firms — showed that this number comes from outdated studies and is actually too low.

When they backtested portfolios from 1968 to 2016, they found that the optimal gold allocation was significantly higher than 10% for most investors — especially those with heavy exposure to stocks and real estate, which tend to fall together in a crisis.

For us specifically: We're 87% in assets that can decline simultaneously (property + stocks). Adding even 10–15% in gold would meaningfully reduce our overall risk and smooth out downturns — without requiring us to sell any existing holdings.

Physical gold vs paper gold — an important distinction

Not all "gold" is equal. The gold price you see quoted is largely determined by paper gold — futures contracts and derivatives where roughly 100 paper ounces are traded for every 1 physical ounce that actually exists. This leverage can distort the price, but it also means that when people rush to hold physical gold, actual bullion becomes far more valuable than the paper price suggests.

Physical gold (coins, bars) is the real thing. ETFs are paper claims. Both track the price, but only physical gold carries zero counterparty risk.

Chapter 7

How do you actually buy gold?

There are a few ways — each with trade-offs between simplicity, cost, and how "real" you want it to feel.

Easiest

Gold ETF

Buy a fund (like GLD or IAU) through any brokerage. Tracks the gold price. No storage needed. Best for a first step.

Physical

Gold coins or bars

Buy actual gold from a reputable dealer (Royal Mint, APMEX, SD Bullion). You own the metal outright — true zero counterparty risk.

Simple

Gold savings account

Services like BullionVault or The Royal Mint let you hold allocated physical gold online with professional vault storage.

Advanced

Gold mining stocks

Buy shares in mining companies. Amplified exposure to gold prices — but adds company risk on top. More volatile than gold itself.

Where to start: A gold ETF through your existing brokerage is the lowest-friction entry point. If you want the real thing, reputable dealers include the Royal Mint (UK), APMEX, and SD Bullion. Avoid unregulated dealers — there are scams in this industry.

Whatever we decide, the goal isn't to get rich from gold — it's to protect what we've already built. Gold is the foundation layer that sits under everything else.
