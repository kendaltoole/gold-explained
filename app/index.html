Chapter 1

What is gold, exactly?

Gold is a metal — but it's also one of the oldest forms of money in human history. For thousands of years, civilizations used gold to trade, pay soldiers, and store wealth. Kings hoarded it. Empires were built on it.

Today, gold doesn't back our currency the way it once did. But it's still treated as a store of value — something people trust to hold its worth over time, especially when other things (like stocks, or even cash) are shaky.

The key idea: Gold is valuable because people believe it's valuable — and they've believed that for 5,000 years. That kind of trust is surprisingly durable.
🏛️

Ancient money

Used as currency since at least 600 BC. The Roman Empire paid its soldiers in gold coins.

🔬

Chemically special

Gold doesn't rust, tarnish, or corrode. A gold coin buried for 2,000 years still looks like gold.

⚖️

Finite supply

All the gold ever mined would fill about 3.5 Olympic swimming pools. You can't just print more.

🌍

Global demand

Jewelry, electronics, central bank reserves — gold has real-world demand that doesn't disappear.

Yes — gold has a price. And it changes every day.

Just like stocks, gold has a live market price that fluctuates constantly. It's quoted in US dollars per troy ounce (about 31 grams). Right now, one ounce of gold is worth roughly:

Gold spot price (approx.)
~$2,900 / oz
Check the live price at kitco.com or Google "gold price"

That price moves based on supply and demand — just like a house, a stock, or a loaf of bread. Here's what pushes it up or down:

📈

Fear & uncertainty push it up

When people are worried about the economy, inflation, or geopolitical instability, they flee to gold. It's the "safe haven" asset.

💵

A weak dollar pushes it up

Gold is priced in dollars. When the dollar weakens, gold gets more expensive in dollar terms — so its price rises.

🏦

Central bank buying pushes it up

Countries like China, Russia, and India have been buying gold aggressively to reduce their dependence on US dollars.

📉

High interest rates can push it down

Gold doesn't pay interest or dividends. When interest rates are high, safe bonds become more attractive, reducing gold's appeal.

Perspective: In 2000, gold was ~$280/oz. In 2024, it hit $2,700+. That's nearly a 10x increase over 24 years — better than a savings account, better than most bonds.
Chapter 3

Why gold matters for our situation

We have a solid base — two properties and some stocks. But our money is concentrated in assets that tend to move together. When the economy struggles, house prices can fall and stocks can fall at the same time.

Gold often does the opposite — it tends to rise when stocks fall and when inflation is high. Adding it to a portfolio is like adding a counterweight.

🛡️

Inflation hedge

When the cost of everything goes up, the purchasing power of cash goes down. Gold tends to hold its value or rise during inflationary periods.

↔️

Low correlation

Gold doesn't usually move in sync with stocks or property. When they zig, gold can zag — smoothing out the overall portfolio.

🔒

No counterparty risk

Physical gold isn't someone else's debt or promise. A stock can go to zero. A bar of gold can't go bankrupt.

🌐

Universally liquid

You can sell gold anywhere in the world. Unlike a house, you don't need months and an estate agent to convert it to cash.

Chapter 5

How do you actually buy gold?

There are a few ways — each with trade-offs between simplicity, cost, and how "real" you want it to feel.

Easiest

Gold ETF

Buy a fund (like GLD or IAU) through any brokerage account. Tracks the gold price. No storage headaches. Most people start here.

Physical

Gold coins or bars

Buy actual gold from a dealer (Royal Mint, APMEX, etc.). You own the metal outright. Need a safe or vault storage. Feels more real.

Simple

Gold savings account

Services like BullionVault or The Royal Mint let you hold allocated gold online. Easy entry, professional storage.

Advanced

Gold mining stocks

Buy shares in gold mining companies. Amplified exposure to gold prices — but also company risk. More volatile.

Where to start: For simplicity, a gold ETF through your existing brokerage is the lowest-friction first step. You can always add physical gold later once you're comfortable with the asset.

Whatever we decide, the goal isn't to get rich from gold — it's to protect what we've already built.
